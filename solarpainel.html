Solar Energy

Solar cells, the building blocks of Solar Panels, consist of layers of semiconductor materials. Silicon is commonly used due to its semiconducting properties. The silicon is doped with specific elements to create a positive and a negative layer.

When sunlight, composed of photons, strikes the solar cells, the energy from the photons is absorbed by the semiconductor material. This absorption causes electrons in the material to become energized and break free from their atomic bonds, creating electron-hole pairs. The positive and negative layers within the solar cell create an electric field, causing these free electrons to move towards the negative layer and the holes towards the positive layer.

The electrons move through an external circuit to recombine with the holes, an electric current is generated. This flow of electrons constitutes the electricity that can be harnessed for various applications. The efficiency of a solar panel is influenced by factors such as the quality of the semiconductor material, the design of the solar cells, and environmental conditions like sunlight intensity and temperature.

Solar panels are interconnected to form solar arrays, allowing for the generation of more substantial amounts of electricity. Inverter systems are used to convert the direct current (DC) produced by the solar panels into alternating current (AC), which is the standard form of electricity used in homes and businesses. The photovoltaic process in solar panels represents a clean and sustainable way to transform solar energy into a usable electrical form.
